Lesson Six

Content Filters

What Is Content Filtering?

Content filtering is a process to screen or restrict access to objectionable emails, webpages, executables, and other suspicious items. It is a common security measure that is often built into internet firewalls and blocks content that contains harmful, illegal, or inappropriate information.

For example, parents often use web filtering to protect their children from improper or graphic material. Organizations use it to block malware-carrying sites and enforce productivity policies.

Four Types of Content Filters

1. Search Engine Filters

Rate web content according to its text and images. Text and images hold a specific weight, measured against a classification set. The weights vary based on whether the classification level is set to off, moderate, or strict.

Machine learning helps define weights to avoid possible false positives. Content is classified as safe, moderate, inappropriate, or rejected. The search engine result will then display contents only if they meet the set classification level.

2. Email Filters

Email content filters check the header of incoming mails against real-time blackhole lists. The raw data of the body is scanned for inappropriate content, providing a spam confidence level similar to search engine weights.

Email content filters also check attachments, identify keywords or potential unauthorized file types (like executables), and complete the email content filtering — enabling users to block, quarantine, or reject malicious emails including phishing, while accepting appropriate incoming emails.

3. DNS-Based Content Filters

DNS-based content filters check the website during the resolution of the domain through DNS servers using blocklists. If the website is not allowed, the browser is redirected to a replacement message announcing that the page is blocked.

Alternatively, a company can define an allowlist of all company-approved websites — DNS-based content filtering would then block all other websites.

4. Web Filters

Web filters are similar to DNS-based content filters with an additional function that categorizes websites. For example, schools in the United States must adhere to the Children's Internet Protection Act (CIPA), addressing children's access to obscene or harmful content. Schools use web filters to block material deemed harmful to minors.

All websites and their contents are rated through machine learning, so access to a specific URL is allowed or blocked according to its category and the user's profile.

Why Content Filtering Matters

Malware & Phishing Protection

Content filters allow organizations to block access to sites known to carry malware, protecting their data and users from malicious activity.

Content filters can also identify phishing or exploit kits, blocking access before it triggers a malicious download. This is important while cybercriminals increasingly develop new, more sophisticated ways to illegally access networks and steal data.

Productivity & Bandwidth

Limiting users' access to only specific work-related internet can increase bandwidth efficiency and enable faster connections for all employees.

Organizations can use web filtering on sites like social media and online shopping to increase staff productivity.

Blocklist
A list of sites/addresses/senders that are explicitly denied. Any entry on the list is blocked by default.
Allowlist
A list of sites/addresses/senders that are explicitly permitted. Everything not on the list is blocked by default.
Real-Time Blackhole List
A list of IP addresses associated with known spam or malicious activity, used to filter email headers.
CIPA
Children's Internet Protection Act — a US law requiring schools and libraries to use web filters to block material harmful to minors in order to receive federal funding.

Lesson Seven

WiFi Security

What Is Wi-Fi?

Wi-Fi, based on the IEEE 802.11 standards, is a wireless technology employed for local area networking of devices. Its development drew from similar protocols and technology as Ethernet, encompassing IP at the network layer and TCP/UDP at the transport layer. Common technologies include routers, switches, DNS, and in most cases, wireless networks connect to wired networks.

The principal advantage of wireless networks is mobility — you can walk around with a device anywhere within Wi-Fi range without being tethered to a cable. The main disadvantage is security — without proper security protocols, bad actors can listen in to communication.

Data is sent through radio waves or microwaves, unlike wired networks that pipe data through twisted copper wire or fiber optic cable.

WiFi Encryption — WEP to WPA3
WEP — Wired Equivalent Privacy (Original)
The first Wi-Fi encryption protocol. Designed to provide security equivalent to wired networks. However, WEP was found to have serious vulnerabilities and was quickly broken by attackers. It was superseded by WPA.
WPA / WPA2 — Wi-Fi Protected Access
WPA2 employs the Advanced Encryption Standard (AES) for encrypting outgoing data — a standard established by the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST). Two security options: Home (personal) level uses network authentication and key exchange relying on a shared passphrase; Enterprise level uses 802.1x authentication mechanisms (like those used in wired networks) to authenticate users and establish encryption. Security remained at risk if poorly chosen or weak passphrases were used at the home level.
WPA3 — Released 2018
WPA3 introduced: a new, more secure handshake for making connections; an easier method for adding devices to the network; increased key sizes; and other security features. While security technology continued to improve, bad actors turned their efforts to social engineering techniques, exploiting human carelessness or naivety.
Wi-Fi Threats & Attack Types

Honeypot / Rogue Access Points

Freely available Wi-Fi in public places comes with risks. Bad actors can set up access points (APs) to act as honeypots in places like coffee shops. Unsuspecting people who connect to these so-called free networks don't realize that the hacker has access to everything they are doing online — account credentials, credit card information, etc.

Be wary, even if a network name seems legitimate.

Evil Twin Attacks

Handheld devices remember networks they've connected to in the past. They automatically look for the network name or SSID (Service Set Identifier) and connect again when they see it.

A hacker can hear your phone looking for the legitimate hotel Wi-Fi you connected to last year, set up a fake AP broadcasting that network name, and trick your device into connecting. These are called evil twin attacks — a type of man-in-the-middle attack used to steal information and infiltrate connecting devices.

Defense: Never trust an SSID with open security. Use other means to encrypt communications — leverage the built-in encryption feature on your device or VPN.
Router & AP Security Best Practices

Setting Up a Secure Router/AP

Change SSID & Default Passphrase
Always change the SSID name and default passphrase that came with the device. Choose a complex passphrase.
Enable Security
Ensure security is enabled on the router so that data is encrypted when sent between devices.
Update Firmware
Update to the latest firmware and select the latest security protocol (e.g., WPA3).
Monitor Connected Devices
Some router/AP software allows monitoring of devices connected to your network — use this feature.
Rogue AP Suppression
Newer APs can detect and suppress rogue APs. Note: there are legal caveats with AP suppression that differ between countries and legal districts — ensure familiarity with legality before implementing.

Strong Encryption Robust Authentication Network Segmentation Guest Network Segmentation Access Control Lists Network Monitoring

Lesson Eight

Endpoint Hardening Techniques

Why Endpoint Hardening Matters

With the spread of IoT devices, the number of endpoints that need to be secured has increased exponentially. There are many strategies and policies to secure not only traditional client and server endpoints, but the newer network-connected devices that have proliferated across all aspects of life.

One of the greatest threats caused by the spread of endpoints: an unsecured device allowing unauthorized access to a network that can be exploited to gather information or compromise other devices.

Four Categories of Endpoint Hardening

Category 1: Administrative Controls

Enforcing secure passwords and restricting user and network access using the Principle of Least Privilege (PoLP).

PoLP: Enforcement of permissions based on need. Users, especially administrators, should only have access to permissions they need to perform their duties.

Category 2: Local Endpoint Protection

Hardening local endpoint protection through a combination of:

OS Security Boot Management Local Disk Encryption DLP Techniques

Category 3: Endpoint Maintenance

Ensuring all devices are:

  • Patched and updated regularly
  • Subject to regular policy checkups
  • Backed up accurately for easy recovery

Category 4: Endpoint Monitoring

Monitoring endpoint devices locally through an EPP (Endpoint Protection Platform) client if available, or over networks using IDS (Intrusion Detection Systems). Also implementing EDR (Endpoint Detection and Response) platforms.

(Covered in detail in Lesson 9)

Passwords & Principle of Least Privilege

Secure Passwords — First Line of Defense

The simplest way to harden and protect endpoints and IoT devices is to ensure the device has a secure password. Household IoT devices regularly ship with a default password that the user is not required to change on installation.

A common first attack strategy: scan a network for devices and attempt to log in using default passwords. Tracking down and enforcing secure passwords on all connected devices is a simple first step that can help reduce overall risk.

Principle of Least Privilege (PoLP)

Another important step: ensure users, especially administrators, have access to only the permissions they need to perform their duties.

Many endpoints, even basic IoT devices, grant users the ability to create administrative roles and permission sets. This allows creation of authenticated roles that limit access to only needed features on a device.

Why it matters: If an attacker gains access to an account with restricted access, it will be much less damaging than if they gain full administrative access. Prevents a weak password or social engineering attack from accidentally granting maximum permissions.
Firmware, BIOS, and Boot Security

What Is Firmware?

Firmware is software that usually runs from a chip on the endpoint. It is responsible for detecting and reporting hardware connected to the device. After performing all hardware checks, firmware assists in loading the operating system.

BIOS
Legacy Basic Input Output System — the older firmware interface for traditional computers and laptops.
UEFI
Unified Extensible Firmware Interface — the newer interface. Usually incorporates a graphical interface and more robust security features. Key advantage: can restrict firmware to load only approved software.

Firmware Security Risks

A frequently overlooked area. Threats that attack firmware and boot processes of endpoint devices have been emerging. Hardening firmware and boot processes is especially important for IoT devices, which lack many of the built-in protections that traditional desktops, laptops, and servers have integrated over the years.

Physical security matters too: physically securing devices so attackers do not have physical access is extremely important. Many devices have an administrator account reset procedure that requires only physical access. Locking down the BIOS and other boot-time systems can prevent these attacks.

Firmware malware attacks: Code is inserted into firmware that causes endpoints to load malicious software or whole new operating systems, compromising other devices. Restricting firmware to only approved software (UEFI feature) is a key defense.
Full Disk Encryption (FDE) & DLP

Full Disk Encryption (FDE)

If a laptop is stolen and the data is not encrypted, it is very easy for the thief to extract useful information. An unencrypted corporate laptop can contain a wealth of useful information about the corporate security posture.

The most common way to secure devices: Full Disk Encryption (FDE) — a software-based solution where the disk is encrypted by the OS. On boot time, the UEFI loads the decrypting information from the OS. Cryptographic keys are stored in a Trusted Platform Module (TPM) and protected by a password or other authentication method.

Because the entire disk is encrypted, if it is stolen, no useful information can be retrieved except by attempting to brute force the drive encryption — which is very costly.

Self-Encrypting Drive (SED)

Another way to implement FDE: use a Self-Encrypting Drive (SED). An SED is a hard drive with a built-in module that automatically handles the encryption and decryption of the contents of the hard drive using instructions from the firmware and OS.

Using an SED pushes the cryptographic effort onto the built-in module in the hard drive, rather than the device CPU and software.

Data Loss Prevention (DLP) on Endpoints

A final way to protect data on an endpoint is to use DLP software. This can detect if someone is trying to copy sensitive information from a device or send it over the network. DLP can block or log the transaction for security.

Another common use of DLP: prevent or limit the use of attachable drives (USB flash drives, external hard drives) to prevent the copying of large amounts of data. DLP can also be network-based, where devices inspect network traffic to alert administrators to keywords or other sensitive information being transmitted over networks.

Note: Many modern devices like smartphones automatically use full disk encryption, but on some devices, this may be disabled by default. Always check if disk encryption and DLP are available on endpoints, especially IoT devices.
Patching, Updates & Backups

Why Patching Matters

Keeping patches up-to-date is critical because identifying and closing potential vulnerabilities is a key step in preventing large-scale cybersecurity attacks. Updating OSs, firmware, and vulnerable software programs and applications is a simple and effective way to reduce overall risk.

Even if a new, unknown attack method compromises a system, having a fully patched infrastructure means further infiltration may be hindered — no other tools in an attacker's toolkit will be effective in pivoting to other systems or collecting and exfiltrating data.

Backup Strategy

Having a comprehensive backup solution for critical endpoints can greatly assist in recovering from cyberattacks or accidents. Back up critical endpoint devices — smartphones, laptops, servers, databases — frequently.

For IoT devices (security cameras, smart locks), having backup equipment that you can configure easily to replace damaged, stolen, or compromised devices should be part of a comprehensive disaster recovery plan.

Ransomware defense: Having a regular backup schedule for all your devices — from computers to cameras — is one of the most effective ways to mitigate a ransomware attack.
"Thorough defense is very important when hardening endpoints. If there are multiple layers of security, it is more difficult to compromise an endpoint and use it to further attack a network."

Lesson Nine

Endpoint Monitoring

The Fourth Category of Endpoint Hardening

Endpoint monitoring is the fourth category in the hardening process. This section includes monitoring of endpoint devices, both locally through an Endpoint Protection Platform (EPP) client if available, or over networks using specialized Network Intrusion Detection Systems (IDS). It is also possible to implement Endpoint Detection and Response (EDR) platforms that can preemptively block new, undiscovered attacks and take immediate action against suspicious files and programs.

Endpoint Solutions — EPP, IDS, and EDR

Endpoint Protection Platform (EPP)

Many companies have created endpoint solutions to help manage and protect various types of endpoints from cyberthreats. Most endpoint solutions support servers, desktops, laptops, and smartphones, with additional plugins and support for the proliferation of new, unknown, and IoT devices.

EPP clients can monitor endpoint behavior locally and report findings to a central management system.

Intrusion Detection System (IDS)

Specialized network intrusion detection systems monitor network traffic for suspicious patterns or known attack signatures. IDS can detect unusual activity — unauthorized scans, suspicious connections, known malware signatures — and generate alerts for security teams.

IDS monitors but does not automatically block; it raises alerts for human review.

Endpoint Detection and Response (EDR)

EDR is a more proactive security solution that constantly scans a device to detect Indicators of Compromise (IoC). If the EDR client detects a suspicious connection, program, or behavior, it can block the action and send an alert. This can help identify and stop threats like ransomware and zero-day attacks that may not have an established signature that would be detected by traditional anti-malware systems.

IoC Detection
EDR leverages AI and large comprehensive databases of known attacks to predict and recognize suspicious files and programs.
Immediate Response
Can trigger alerts to other connected endpoints and allow other endpoints to immediately block the suspicious program or file, even before it can be opened or executed.
Zero-day & Unknown Attacks
Provides an immediate response against zero-day and other previously unidentified attacks that traditional anti-malware cannot detect.
Investigation Tools
EDR systems have tools to help security investigators gather data on new threats and quarantine systems suspected of compromise.
Hardening Unknown & Hard-to-Secure Devices

Isolate Hard-to-Secure Devices

With hard-to-secure devices and unknown endpoints, enforce the principle of least privilege. If these devices need access only to a specific internet or internal server, isolate them on a unique network and allow only that specific connection through firewalls and routers.

That way, if the device does not meet network compliance because it is not running an appropriate endpoint security solution, it has as limited access to other resources as possible.

Lock Down Known Devices

Once all known devices are registered, configure many network security devices — wireless access points, switches, routers, firewalls, and other connectivity points — to lock down and not allow unauthorized devices to connect through the network.

Disabling ports for devices that are not monitored forces users with unknown devices to register and prevents attackers from attempting to insert their own devices onto the network remotely or by physically plugging in a device locally.

EPP
Endpoint Protection Platform — monitors and protects endpoints, supports servers, desktops, laptops, smartphones, and IoT devices.
IDS
Intrusion Detection System — monitors network traffic for suspicious patterns; generates alerts but does not automatically block.
EDR
Endpoint Detection and Response — proactive; scans for IoC, can block threats and alert other endpoints; uses AI/ML; effective against zero-day attacks.
IoC
Indicator of Compromise — evidence (file, behavior, connection) suggesting that a system may have been breached or is under attack.

Lesson Ten

SOAR

What Is SOAR?

SOAR (Security Orchestration, Automation, and Response) connects tools in your security stack together into defined workflows, which can run automatically. SOAR lets you increase your team's efficiency by automating repetitive manual processes.

SOAR is a hot term in the security industry. It is essential because security teams are overwhelmed — as new tools are developed to address an evolving threat landscape, analysts must switch between them to accomplish day-to-day tasks.

The Problem SOAR Solves — Alert Fatigue

Alert Fatigue

One common day-to-day task is responding to alerts. With more security tools come more alerts, which are addressed through a series of manual processes and context switches — switching from one tool to another.

Having more alerts to respond to each day means less time to spend on each alert, which increases the likelihood of making mistakes. Performance degradation in the face of a flood of alerts is called "alert fatigue."

One way to mitigate alert fatigue is to hire more analysts — however, there just aren't enough qualified analysts to hire. SOAR offers an alternative solution.

How SOAR Reduces Alert Fatigue

SOAR ties together the tools in your security stack. By pulling data from these sources, SOAR reduces the amount of context switching analysts have to deal with. Analysts can perform all their usual investigative processes directly from the source interface.

Additionally, you can automatically or manually translate processes into a playbook — a flowchart-like set of steps that can be repeated on demand. By using a playbook, you ensure every step in your standard operating procedure is followed, and you have access to data about exactly who did what and when. This capability is called orchestration and automation.

Three Core SOAR Capabilities

1. Orchestration & Automation

SOAR connects all security tools into a unified workbench. Processes are translated into playbooks that can run automatically. This replaces repetitive, time-consuming manual processes with automation at machine speed — freeing analysts to focus on investigating critical alerts.

2. Investigation

Another crucial SOAR capability. When a suspicious alert appears, teams can perform investigative tasks — checking threat intelligence sources for a reputation, or querying a Security Information Management (SIM) system for related events — from within the SOAR platform. Findings determine required mitigation steps.

3. Response & Collaboration

Since SOAR is a unified workbench of all security tools, mitigation steps can also be taken from within it. Example: block traffic from a malicious IP in your firewall, or delete a phishing email from your email server.

SOAR allows users to assign alerts to different analysts or teams at different stages of the response process, and allows assigned users to add information to alerts as they work on them — so anyone who references those alerts later has additional context.

Playbooks — SOAR's Key Mechanism

What Is a Playbook?

Teams use playbooks (sometimes called workflows) to respond to alerts or incidents the same way every time. Playbooks work in unison with security teams by taking the steps an analyst would typically implement when responding to an incident.

Repetitive Tasks
Playbooks do the repetitive tasks — compiling data into a report, sending emails — automatically.
Human Oversight
Playbooks can pause when human oversight is needed (e.g., when a firewall block is being implemented).
Consistency
Ensures every step in the standard operating procedure is followed, improving response speed and consistency.
Reduced Error
Ultimately can lead to reduced analyst workload and reduced chance of error.

Phishing Investigation Playbook — Real-World Example

Phishing investigations are one of the most common use cases for SOAR. Without SOAR, an analyst must manually investigate the sender, key indicators in the email headers or body, enter domains and URLs into a threat intelligence platform, investigate their email server and SIM, determine who received the email, determine who clicked on it, delete it, and so on.

With a SOAR phishing investigation playbook:

  1. Initial investigation steps are taken automatically as soon as the phishing email is reported.
  2. The analyst is alerted only to emails that the playbook determines are suspicious.
  3. After the analyst confirms the email warrants further action, the playbook continues — making additional SIM queries, deleting the email from all user inboxes, sending an email to all recipients alerting them of the action taken, and providing helpful tips on what to do if they receive similar phishing messages in the future.

SOAR Business Benefits

Implementing SOAR into your ecosystem does more than just centralize incident response processes — it optimizes an entire operation. Optimization results in:

Streamlined responses at machine speed Improved team collaboration Better alert fatigue management Consistent standard operating procedures Full audit trail (who did what, when) Faster incident resolution
