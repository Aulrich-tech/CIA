Lesson Eleven

SIEM — Security Information and Event Management

What Is SIEM?

Introduced in 2005, SIEM (Security Information and Event Management) analyzes security alerts in real time. It is a technology that has evolved from a simple log aggregator into a fully integrated and automated center for security and network operations.

Three Things SIEM Does

1 — Collect & Store

Collect, normalize, and store events and alerts from the organization's network and security devices, servers, databases, applications, and endpoints in a secure, central location.

SIEM collects information from physical and virtual devices, both on-premises and in the cloud. Logging in to every system individually was increasingly impossible — and unsecured logs could be deleted by attackers to hide their activities.

2 — Analyze

Run advanced analytics on data — both in real time and across historical data — to identify potential security incidents to be investigated by a human. Potential incidents are prioritized by risk, severity, and impact.

Security analytics have grown from simple cross-correlation rules to monitoring for user-behavioral anomalies, watching for known Indicators of Compromise (IoC), and applying sophisticated machine learning models.

3 — Prove Compliance

Prove that all the security controls under the purview of the SIEM are in place and effective. The primary driver for purchasing SIEM has been regulatory compliance.

PCI Standard Sarbanes-Oxley HIPAA GDPR

Businesses, hospitals, and other organizations that ignore compliance requirements face punitive fines.

SIEM Evolution
Phase 1 — Information Platform
Early SIEM centralized logs for compliance. It was the primary driver for purchasing. Organizations needed proof that security controls were in place. However, the technology was complex, difficult to tune, and demanded a high level of skill from users.
Phase 2 — Threat Intelligence Center
As cyberattacks became stealthier and more sophisticated, demands for information about attacks grew more urgent. Security teams very often did not discover breaches until many months after they occurred — and more often by a third party than internally. SIEM vendors added threat detection: built-in threat intelligence, historical and real-time analytics, and User and Entity Behavior Analytics (UEBA). Machine learning became part of the toolkit, especially for sifting through big data.
Phase 3 — Integrated NOC & SOC (Modern SIEM)
Recent SIEM development integrates NOC (Network Operations Center) and SOC (Security Operations Center), establishing SIEM as the nerve center of all network and security operations. From a single pane of glass, IT security gains visibility into the entire network. SIEM uses a self-learning, real-time asset discovery and device configuration engine that builds a topology of all connected objects. By establishing a baseline of normal behavior, machine learning can alert analysts to abnormal activity before a breach occurs.
Challenges & Limitations

What Held SIEM Back

SIEM was not a set-and-forget technology. Two compounding problems: (1) IT security suffers from an insufficient number of qualified professionals; (2) the siloed approach used in typical NOCs and SOCs increases complexity and causes a lack of network visibility.

An environment of multivendor, single-point solutions with different operating systems, patch cycles, protocols, and logic worked counter to interoperability. The result: greater demand on sparse IT resources, increased human error, and reduced network security visibility.

More Automation — The Solution

The systemic shortage of trained personnel was the impetus for more automation and machine learning in later SIEM devices. AI more quickly detects trends and patterns in enormous payloads of data than even the cleverest human.

Moreover, time and accuracy are gained by configuring SIEM to automatically respond and remediate. SIEM evolved from an information platform → a threat intelligence center → a fully integrated and automated center for security and network operations.

Key Terms
SIEM
Security Information and Event Management — introduced 2005; collects, normalizes, stores, and analyzes security events in real time; proves compliance; evolved into a unified NOC/SOC platform.
UEBA
User and Entity Behavior Analytics — monitors behavior of users and entities to detect anomalies that could indicate a security incident.
NOC
Network Operations Center — monitors and manages network infrastructure for performance and availability.
SOC
Security Operations Center — monitors, detects, and responds to cybersecurity incidents in real time.
IoC
Indicators of Compromise — evidence that a system has been breached or attacked; detected by SIEM analytics.
Single Pane of Glass
A unified management interface providing full visibility into the entire network and security environment from one place.

SD-WAN — Software-Defined Wide Area Network

What Is SD-WAN?

SD-WAN (Software-Defined Wide Area Network) is a technology that uses software-defined networking (SDN) principles to improve how wide-area networks (WANs) connect across different locations. It helps organizations link users, apps, and data securely, providing better performance and making network management easier through centralized control.

As the use of business-critical, cloud-based applications continues to increase, organizations with distributed infrastructure and an expanding remote workforce need to adapt. The most effective solution is to switch from static, performance-inhibited traditional WANs to SD-WAN architectures.

How SD-WAN Works

Traffic Monitoring & Routing

SD-WAN monitors the performance of WAN connections and regulates various types of traffic to ensure consistently high speeds and optimize overall connectivity. It allows application-aware traffic routing and enables fine-tuning such as reserving costly high-speed links for business-critical applications.

Unlike the traditional router-centric WAN structure, the SD-WAN model fully supports applications located in on-premises data centers, public or private clouds, and SaaS services like Salesforce, Dropbox, AWS, and others.

Six Advantages of SD-WAN

Centralized Orchestration

SD-WAN centrally manages deployments, configurations, and operations. Network administrators can generate and modify security rules in real time according to evolving network demands — reducing complexity and resources needed to manage networks.

Direct Cloud Access

Eliminates the need for backhauling. Enables direct access to critical cloud services for all users regardless of location — no need to route branch office traffic through a data center regardless of the actual destination.

Better App Performance

Can be configured to prioritize business-critical traffic and real-time services like VoIP, steering it over the most efficient route. Reduces packet loss from overloaded circuits and latency due to heavy traffic.

Increased Business Agility

Network planners can deploy updates to all networks simultaneously, allowing organizations to respond to changing business needs swiftly and without delay.

Cost Saving

Routes traffic efficiently over multiple channels including existing MPLS circuits and the public internet over LTE and broadband. Reduces the need to increase bandwidth on costly links.

Improved Security

Built-in security features and access restrictions protect against internal and external threats. Recommended to use SD-WAN solutions with integrated NGFW, IPS, encryption, AV, and sandboxing capabilities.

Three Phases of SD-WAN Evolution
Phase 1 — Need for Higher Bandwidth
SD-WAN was developed as a response to the high cost and limited bandwidth of the traditional WAN. A workaround: add multiple dedicated carrier links and load-balance per-application traffic based on available bandwidth. Adding multiple products from multiple vendors — each with a different management console — significantly increased infrastructure complexity. Still, the first generation solved a pressing need: basic load-balancing techniques allowed networks to make application-intelligent decisions on hybrid WAN links (service provider, broadband, LTE).
Phase 2 — Need for High Performance
New capabilities such as virtualization failover/failback and application-aware routing were developed, driven by demand for enhanced performance and agility. This addressed delays associated with MPLS installation. Virtualization enabled network administrators to manage paths from a unified control panel, simplifying operational processes.
Phase 3 — Need for Unified Security Infrastructure
SD-WAN technology evolved beyond connecting remote branch offices to a unified infrastructure for cloud, mobility, and "as-a-service" technologies by integrating security and networking functionalities into a single, secure SD-WAN appliance. Businesses can now replace multiple-point products with one powerful security appliance — at reduced cost and with increased ease of management. This is the basis for Secure SD-WAN.

Secure SD-WAN (Fortinet)

Secure SD-WAN is the combination of a firewall and SD-WAN functions in one device — providing increased network security and simplified network administrator tasks. Fortinet introduced the term "Secure SD-WAN."

Application Management
Prioritizes business-critical applications and allocates bandwidth per operational requirements, reducing operational cost.
Single Pane of Glass
Centralized management console provides visibility and telemetry to identify, troubleshoot, and resolve network issues with minimal IT staff.
Comprehensive Analytics
Analytics on bandwidth utilization, application definition, path selection, and the security threat landscape help admins quickly redesign policies based on historical statistics.
Key Terms
SD-WAN
Software-Defined Wide Area Network — uses SDN principles to improve WAN connectivity with application-aware routing and centralized management.
WAN
Wide Area Network — connects networks across large geographic areas, such as branch offices to headquarters.
MPLS
Multiprotocol Label Switching — a traditional, expensive WAN technology that SD-WAN can supplement or replace with cheaper broadband or LTE links.

Zero Trust Network Access (ZTNA)

What Is ZTNA?

ZTNA (Zero Trust Network Access) establishes a secure session between an end entity and a network, while ensuring granular control over access to resources and exercising zero trust, regardless of the location of either the end entity or the network.

As a network security concept, zero trust operates under the premise that no user or device inside or outside the network should be trusted, unless their identification and security status have been thoroughly checked. Zero trust assumes that threats — both outside and inside the network — are omnipresent, and that every attempt to access a network or application is a threat.

Core Zero Trust Principles

Least Privilege Access

Part of the zero trust principle is the practice of least privilege access: users are only granted access to the resources necessary to fulfill their job requirements — and no more.

This limits the potential damage if a user's credentials are compromised, since the attacker can only access what that user was permitted to access.

Always Verify — Never Trust

Regardless of whether the end entity is remote or on-premises, the connecting computing device automatically establishes an encrypted session with the network.

This connection takes place between a ZTNA client at the end entity and the ZTNA access proxy (e.g., a firewall). The proxy hides the locations of requested applications from the outside, and only directs the client's request to the application if the user meets access requirements.

ZTNA Components
ZTNA Client
Software on the end entity's device that initiates the encrypted connection to the ZTNA access proxy. In Fortinet's implementation: FortiClient.
ZTNA Access Proxy
Typically a firewall (e.g., FortiGate). Receives the encrypted session, verifies device identity and security posture, and directs requests to the appropriate application. Hides application locations from the outside world.
ZTNA Policy Server
Enforces policy-to-control access to applications. In Fortinet's implementation: FortiClient EMS (Endpoint Management Server). Policies can include geolocation — if a device connects from an unexpected location, access can be denied or privileges reduced.
Authentication Server
The user is identified via authentication against an Active Directory, LDAP directory, database, or IDaaS (Identity-as-a-Service). Policy is then applied based on the user's roles.
ZTNA vs. VPN: Unlike IPsec VPN (an open standard), ZTNA is vendor-specific — like SSL VPN. Each vendor implements ZTNA in a way that best suits their requirements.
How Fortinet ZTNA Works — Step by Step
Step 1 — Device Registration via FortiClient EMS
When an endpoint device with FortiClient connects to the network for the first time, it is directed to FortiClient EMS to register. During registration, FortiClient provides the server with information about the device, the user, and the security posture of the device. This information is written to tags and shared with FortiGate.
Step 2 — Tag-Based Rules Applied by FortiGate
Based on the information in the tags, the device can be grouped and certain rules applied. The rules act as instructions for FortiGate each time the device connects. Example: a device with Windows 10 + antivirus is allowed access; a device with Windows 10 and no antivirus is denied access.
Step 3 — Digital Certificate Issued
At the end of registration, FortiClient EMS generates a digital certificate for the device, sends it to the device, and shares it with FortiGate. From this point, the device submits the certificate to FortiGate each time it needs to identify itself.
Step 4 — Continuous Network Telemetry
FortiClient is in continuous communication with FortiClient EMS. If endpoint information changes (e.g., antivirus is disabled), the server updates the client tags and resynchronizes with FortiGate. This ongoing communication is called network telemetry — providing agile and dynamic responses to enhance network security.
Step 5 — Encrypted Session Established (or Denied)
When the endpoint connects to the ZTNA access proxy, FortiGate challenges for device identification (certificate), then user authentication (credentials). Credentials are sent to the backend for authentication. The ZTNA access proxy retrieves the user's identity and role. If device tags, rules, and user roles all allow access, an encrypted session is initiated and the user gains access to the application.
Key Terms
Zero Trust
Security principle: no user or device inside or outside the network should be trusted unless their identification and security status have been thoroughly checked.
ZTNA
Zero Trust Network Access — establishes secure, encrypted sessions while enforcing granular, role-based access control regardless of user or device location.
Network Telemetry
Continuous communication between the ZTNA client (FortiClient) and policy server (FortiClient EMS) to keep device tags and security posture current and synchronized with the firewall.
IDaaS
Identity-as-a-Service — cloud-based identity management and authentication used by ZTNA to verify user identity.

Users and devices are granted only the minimum permissions necessary to perform their role — limiting damage if credentials are compromised.

Lesson Fourteen

Cloud Security

How the Cloud Was Born

Before the cloud, organizations purchased their own computer systems to run application software. These were located in the organization's facilities and managed by teams of experts — often more than one server per major application. Most systems were idle most of the time, waiting for the next transaction: wasted resources.

Virtualization — technology from mainframe computing — lets a single server run operating systems and applications from multiple servers at the same time, consolidating workloads onto fewer servers and saving money. Entrepreneurs then built enormous data centers filled with generalized hardware and offered to rent out portions of this infrastructure. With that, the cloud was born.

Three Cloud Service Models

IaaS

Infrastructure-as-a-Service — provides organizations with networking, storage, physical servers, and virtualization. Users must still provide operating systems, middleware, data, and applications.

Used when demand varies — e.g., seasonal holidays increasing workloads.

Amazon Web Services Microsoft Azure Google Cloud Platform

PaaS

Platform-as-a-Service — service providers rent cloud-based platforms for software developers to develop and deliver applications. Provides the OS and middleware in addition to IaaS elements.

Makes it easier, more efficient, and cheaper to build, test, and deploy applications.

SaaS

Software-as-a-Service — the software is hosted by a third party. The end user typically connects using their browser.

Google Mail Salesforce DocuSign Netflix

Moving from expensive capital assets to a recurring operating cost model is very attractive to most organizations.

Shared Responsibility Model

Security Is Divided

When applications are hosted in a company's own data center, the security picture is straightforward. In the cloud, however, security is a shared responsibility between the cloud provider and the customer.

From an availability perspective, the infrastructure is designed by the vendor to be highly available — infrastructure uptime is the vendor's responsibility. From a security perspective, the vendor is responsible only for securing the infrastructure it provides.

As a customer, when you install virtualized applications in the vendor's cloud infrastructure, you are responsible for securing the access, network traffic, and data applications.

The Multicloud Problem

Many organizations operate in a hybrid world where some applications remain hosted in their own data centers, some in Vendor-A IaaS, some in Vendor-B, and others with multiple SaaS vendors.

This is called a "multicloud" environment — and it comes with a multicloud problem: multiple, independent, uncoordinated security solutions. Complexity scales geometrically with the number of cloud vendors involved.

Additional challenge: Vendor-supplied security tools tend to provide only a few basic security functions — the same tools used to secure the underlying infrastructure. If an attacker bypasses them at the infrastructure layer, they would likely bypass them at the customer's application level as well.

Fortinet's Answer

Fortinet has a portfolio of security solutions that are at home in a company's data center, providing the same consistent security, and also optimized for all the leading IaaS cloud providers. This allows organizations to maintain uniform security policies across on-premises and multicloud environments.

Key Terms
Virtualization
Technology that lets a single server run operating systems and applications from multiple servers simultaneously — the foundation of cloud computing.
IaaS
Infrastructure-as-a-Service — vendor provides networking, storage, servers, and virtualization. Customer provides OS, middleware, data, and applications.
PaaS
Platform-as-a-Service — vendor provides IaaS elements plus OS and middleware. Customer provides data and applications.
SaaS
Software-as-a-Service — vendor provides and hosts the full application. Customer accesses via browser.
Middleware
Software that acts as a bridge between the operating system and applications.
Shared Responsibility Model
In cloud security: vendor secures the infrastructure; customer secures their access, network traffic, and applications running on it.
Multicloud
An environment using multiple cloud providers simultaneously. Creates a security coordination challenge where complexity scales geometrically.

SASE — Secure Access Service Edge

What Is SASE?

SASE (Secure Access Service Edge) technology addresses the needs of complex network infrastructure by combining Network-as-a-Service with Security-as-a-Service capabilities. Delivered through the cloud as an as-a-service consumption model, SASE supports secure access for today's distributed and hybrid enterprise networks.

SASE is composed of Security Service Edge (SSE) and SD-WAN. It lets work-from-anywhere and remote workers take advantage of Firewall-as-a-Service (FWaaS), Secure Web Gateway (SWG), Zero Trust Network Access (ZTNA), and a medley of threat detection functions.

Why SASE Was Needed

The Modern Access Problem

As work environments have evolved, so too have user behavior and endpoint protection requirements. Organizations today require that their users have immediate, continuous secure access to network and cloud-based resources and data — regardless of location, on any device, and at any time.

Organizations must provide this access in a scalable and elastic way that integrates thin edge network sites and remote users into the central infrastructure, and that favors a lean operational, as-a-service model.

What SASE Delivers

When properly implemented, SASE allows organizations to apply secure access no matter where their users, workloads, devices, or applications are located. This is critically important for remote worker security.

Flexible & Consistent Security Reduced IT Cost & Complexity Fast, Seamless User Experience Cloud-Delivered at Scale
Six Key Features of SASE

1 — SD-WAN

Employs an overlay structure to streamline operations and enhance user satisfaction by intelligently directing traffic to the most efficient routes for internet, cloud applications, and data centers. Facilitates swift deployment of new applications and services while efficiently managing policies across numerous locations.

2 — Secure Web Gateway (SWG)

Provides a secure web experience to protect users, devices, and applications from online threats. Filters malware, prevents data loss, and enforces internet policy compliance.

3 — Firewall-as-a-Service (FWaaS)

Enables the substitution of physical firewall appliances with cloud-based counterparts, offering advanced NGFW security capabilities — IPS, Anti-Malware, SSL Inspection, and Sandbox.

4 — CASB

Cloud Access Security Broker — prevents data leaks, malware infection, regulatory noncompliance, and lack of visibility by ensuring safe use of cloud apps and services. Secures cloud applications hosted in public clouds (IaaS), private clouds, or delivered as SaaS.

5 — ZTNA

Provides secure access to internal applications for remote users. Ensures no user or device is automatically trusted. Every attempt to access a system — from inside or outside — is challenged and verified before granting access. Consists of multiple technologies: MFA, secure NAC, and access policy enforcement.

6 — Centralized Management

Centralizing management of all security features within a single console enables streamlining of change control, patch management, coordination of outage windows, and policy administration — ensuring consistent policies across the organization regardless of where users connect from.

SASE Architecture — SSE + SD-WAN

SASE = SSE + SD-WAN

SSE (Security Service Edge)
The security component of SASE — includes SWG, CASB, FWaaS, and ZTNA. Delivered from the cloud to protect users accessing the internet, SaaS, and private applications.
SD-WAN
The networking component of SASE — connects distributed sites and users with optimized, application-aware routing and centralized management.

FortiSASE (Fortinet)

Fortinet's SASE solution — FortiSASE — seamlessly integrates essential networking and security technologies delivered through the cloud. Its innovative approach ensures secure access for a hybrid workforce and safeguards applications and data on any cloud.

FortiSASE integrates cloud-delivered SD-WAN connectivity with SSE, extending the convergence of networking and security from the edge to remote users. Fortinet is the first vendor to deliver a comprehensive SASE solution.

"SASE addresses the reality that users, workloads, devices, and applications can be located anywhere — security must follow the user, not be tied to a physical perimeter."
